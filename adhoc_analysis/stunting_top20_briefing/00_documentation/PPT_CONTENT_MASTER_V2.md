### Slide 4 - Key Messages

- Slide type: bullet slide
- On-slide text:
  1. An estimated 150.2 million children under 5 were stunted in 2024.
     The top 20 countries account for over three quarters of the global total.
  2. The global number of stunted children fell by 51.7 million over two
     decades, and India alone accounted for more than half of that reduction.
  3. The highest-prevalence countries are concentrated in Sub-Saharan Africa,
     while the highest-burden countries span Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia.
  4. Progress has been narrowly concentrated in a small number of populous
     countries.
- Speaker notes:
  These four messages summarise the analytical story. The burden is highly
  concentrated both in current levels and in where progress has occurred.
  The distinction between prevalence and burden is a recurring theme
  throughout the deck.

### Slide 8 - Concentration and Overlap

- Slide type: bullet slide
- On-slide text:
  1. The burden is highly concentrated: the top 5 countries accounted for
     50.1% of the global total, the top 10 for 62.4%, and the top 20 for 76.8%.
  2. India alone accounted for 37.4 million stunted children, or 24.9%
     of the global total.
  3. Eleven countries appear in both the top-20 highest-prevalence and top-20
     highest-burden lists: Afghanistan, Angola, the Democratic Republic of the
     Congo, Ethiopia, Madagascar, Mozambique, Niger, Nigeria, Pakistan, Sudan,
     and Yemen.
- Speaker notes:
  The burden is highly concentrated. Targeting the overlap countries can
  enable an equity-focused approach while also reaching the settings where
  absolute impact can be largest. Countries with very high prevalence but
  smaller populations may not appear among the highest-burden countries,
  while populous countries with moderate prevalence can carry a very large
  burden.

### Slide 14 - Reduction Concentration

- Slide type: bullet slide
- On-slide text:
  1. The top 5 burden reducers accounted for 91.3% of the global 20-year
     reduction, and the top 10 for 100.9%.
  2. The top-10 share exceeds 100% because burden increased in some countries,
     particularly in African countries with high population growth and little
     or no improvement in prevalence.
  3. The top 20 reducers accounted for 111.3%, reinforcing that progress has
     been driven by a small number of populous countries while burden grew
     elsewhere.
- Speaker notes:
  This concentration is striking. Progress in reducing the global number of
  stunted children has been even more concentrated than current burden itself,
  with a handful of populous countries driving the bulk of progress. That the
  top-10 share exceeds 100% is not an error — it reflects the fact that burden
  increased in some countries over this period, so the largest reducers had to
  more than offset the global net decline.
- Chart/table: none

### Slide 15 - Limitations

- Slide type: bullet slide
- On-slide text:
  1. These rankings use JME modeled stunting estimates, which produce smoothed
     trajectories rather than direct annual observations.
  2. Acute shocks — including conflict, economic crisis, and food emergencies
     — may not be captured in real time by the modeled series. Estimates and
     rankings should not be over-interpreted.
- Speaker notes:
  The modeled estimates are appropriate for trend comparison but should not be
  treated as observed annual values. Acute disruptions may take time to appear
  in the smoothed series.
