## Working Headline

Modeled stunting remains highly concentrated in a small group of countries, and
the countries with the highest prevalence are not identical to those carrying
the largest burden.

## Zero Draft For 1-Page Brief

### Executive Summary

Modelled JME estimates for 2024 show that very high stunting prevalence remains
concentrated in a group of countries led by Burundi at 55.3 per cent, followed
by Niger at 48.3 per cent, Eritrea at 48.0 per cent, Angola at 47.7 per cent,
and Papua New Guinea at 47.6 per cent. The burden profile is different: India
has by far the largest estimated number of stunted children at 37.4 million,
followed by Nigeria at 11.4 million, Pakistan at 10.7 million, the Democratic
Republic of the Congo at 8.7 million, and Ethiopia at 6.9 million.

The overlap between the two views is substantial but not complete. Eleven
countries appear in both the top-20 prevalence and top-20 burden lists:
Niger, Angola, Yemen, the Democratic Republic of the Congo, Afghanistan,
Madagascar, Mozambique, Ethiopia, Sudan, Nigeria, and Pakistan. This suggests
that leadership should distinguish between countries where stunting intensity is
extreme and countries where the absolute number of affected children is driven
by population scale as well as prevalence.

Progress is visible but uneven. Over 2014-2024, the largest prevalence
reductions were recorded in Libya at 13.9 percentage points, Comoros at
11.1 percentage points, and Nepal at 11.0 percentage points. Over 2004-2024,
the largest reductions were recorded in North Korea at 27.6 percentage points,
Nepal at 25.3 percentage points, and Tajikistan at 24.9 percentage points.
At the same time, high burden remains concentrated in very populous countries:
the top five burden countries account for 75.2 million of the 115.3 million
children represented across the top 20 burden countries in the current output.

### Key Messages

- The prevalence ranking and burden ranking should be read together rather than
  treated as interchangeable views of the problem.
- A relatively small set of populous countries carries most of the burden among
  the top 20 burden countries, even though the highest-prevalence countries are
  partly different.
- Some countries have achieved large declines in prevalence over 10-year and
  20-year windows, but those rankings do not by themselves explain whether the
  number of affected children fell at the same pace.
- The current prepared input supports 162-country comparison universes for the
  latest-year, 10-year, and 20-year rankings used here.

### Draft Closing Paragraph

The immediate implication for leadership is that the 2024 stunting picture has
to be read through both severity and scale. The highest-prevalence countries
identify where modeled stunting rates remain most extreme, while the burden
ranking shows where the largest numbers of children are affected. The current
results also show that strong long-run progress is possible, but they do not
support stronger claims about cause, programme effect, or the contribution of
population growth unless those relationships are computed explicitly in the next
analytical step.

## Zero Draft For 2-Page Brief

### Section 1 - What The Current Data Show

The current approved briefing outputs use modelled national stunting estimates
for children under 5 and identify 2024 as the latest year available in the
prepared input. On prevalence, Burundi, Niger, Eritrea, Angola, and Papua New
Guinea currently lead the ranking. On burden, the picture shifts sharply toward
large-population countries, with India, Nigeria, Pakistan, the Democratic
Republic of the Congo, and Ethiopia accounting for the largest estimated
numbers of stunted children.

This difference matters for interpretation. A country can rank very high on
prevalence without contributing one of the largest absolute burdens, and a
country can carry a very large burden even when its prevalence is lower than
the most extreme-rate countries. Eleven countries appear on both top-20 lists,
which makes them particularly important for leadership attention because both
rate and scale remain high in the current modeled output.

### Section 2 - Where Progress Has Been Strongest

The strongest 10-year prevalence reductions in the current output are Libya,
Comoros, and Nepal. The strongest 20-year reductions are North Korea, Nepal,
and Tajikistan. These results show that large absolute declines are present in
the series, but the ranking is endpoint-based and should be interpreted as a
comparison of two time points rather than a full trajectory assessment.

Burden change also matters. India records the largest reduction in the estimated
number of stunted children over both 2014-2024 and 2004-2024, yet it still
remains the country with the largest burden in 2024. Pakistan and Nigeria also
remain among the highest-burden countries in 2024 even though both appear in
the burden-reduction rankings. This is a useful warning against assuming that
falling prevalence automatically removes countries from the highest-burden
group.

### Section 3 - What Should Be Said Carefully

The current results support careful synthesis, not causal claims. They support
statements about relative ranking, overlap, concentration, and measured change
over the selected windows. They do not yet support stronger statements about
why some countries improved more than others, whether conflict or other shocks
fully explain current patterns, or how much residual burden reflects child
population growth unless an explicit decomposition is added.

The modeled series is suitable for trend interpretation, but it is smoothed and
should not be read as a direct year-by-year record of sudden change. Rapid
shocks, including conflict and war, may not be reflected immediately in the
modeled series. Final note and slide text should therefore keep the language
descriptive and avoid implying real-time responsiveness or unsupported causal
mechanisms.
