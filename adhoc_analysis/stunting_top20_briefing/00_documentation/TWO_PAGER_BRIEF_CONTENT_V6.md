# Child Stunting: Where Levels Remain Highest and Where Progress Has Been Strongest

[[SUBTITLE: UNICEF Combined Nutrition Database, 2026 | Indicator: Stunting (modeled height-for-age < -2 SD), children under 5]]

[[PAGE2_KICKER: GLOBAL NUTRITION MONITORING BRIEF]]

---

## Key Messages

- An estimated 150.2 million children under 5 were stunted in 2024. There is high concentration: the 20 countries with the largest number of stunted children account for over three quarters of the global total.
- The highest-prevalence countries are concentrated in Sub-Saharan Africa, while the largest absolute burdens are concentrated in high-population countries in Southern Asia and Sub-Saharan Africa.
- The global number of stunted children fell by 51.7 million over the past two decades, and the five countries with the largest reductions accounted for 91% of the global reduction.
- Global under-five population fell by 5.3% between 2012 and 2024, and in many countries, population decline accelerated the reduction in the number of stunted children.
- In contrast to global trends, child population grew by 20.3% in Sub-Saharan Africa from 2012 to 2024, and for some countries the number of stunted children rose or remained flat even when prevalence declined.

---

## Current Scale: Prevalence & Burden

Burundi had the highest modeled stunting prevalence in 2024 at 55.3%, followed by Niger, Eritrea, Angola, and Papua New Guinea. The highest-prevalence countries remain concentrated in Sub-Saharan Africa, with one Pacific country in the top five. (Figure 1a). The 20 highest-prevalence countries were all classified as "very high" according to WHO/UNICEF prevalence thresholds.

India had the largest estimated number of stunted children in 2024 at 37.4 million – one in every four stunted children globally. At the regional level, Southern Asia remained the single largest concentration of affected children at 56.4 million, while regions in Sub-Saharan Africa had 59.6 million in total.

The burden is highly concentrated. In 2024, the five largest-number countries accounted for 50% of the global total, the largest 10 for 62%, and the largest 20 for 77%. All 10 largest-number countries also had "high" or "very high" prevalence (Figure 1b).

## Progress: Reduction in Prevalence and Number of Affected Children Over 20 Years

Figures 2a and 2b show countries with the largest reductions in stunting prevalence and numbers affected from 2004 to 2024. North Korea had the largest 20-year reduction in stunting prevalence, with a decline of 27.6 percentage points. The average prevalence reduction for the five largest-reduction countries was 1.3 percentage points per year. Albania managed to reduce prevalence by 21.1 points and move from "high" prevalence to "low" prevalence over the 20 years (Figure 2a).

The global number of stunted children fell from 201.9 million in 2004 to 150.2 million in 2024 – a reduction of 51.7 million. India alone accounted for 27.5 million of that reduction, or 53.3% of the global total, yet it remains the single largest-burden country and is still in the "very high" prevalence category. China moved from "medium" prevalence to "low" prevalence, reducing the number of stunted children by 9.5 million over the 20 years (Figure 2b).

The concentration of numbers affected is striking: the five largest-reduction countries accounted for 91.3% of the global reduction, and the highest-10 for 100.9%. That the largest-10 share exceeds 100% is not an error – it reflects the fact that the number of stunted children increased in some countries over this period, particularly in African countries that had high population growth and little or no improvement in prevalence.

## Interpreting the Results

The prevalence and burden rankings overlap but are not identical. 11 countries appear in both the 20 highest-prevalence and 20 largest-burden lists: Afghanistan, Angola, D.R. Congo, Ethiopia, Madagascar, Mozambique, Niger, Nigeria, Pakistan, Sudan, and Yemen. Targeting countries with both high prevalence and high burden can enable an equity focused approach that also achieves impact at-scale.

Population change is central to interpretation. The global under-five population fell from 683.5 million in 2012 to 647.3 million in 2024, but it rose from 154.0 million to 185.4 million in Sub-Saharan Africa. Population trends help explain why prevalence gains translate unevenly into burden reduction. For example, In Western Africa, prevalence fell from 33.7% to 29.7% from 2012 to 2024 while the number of stunted children remained almost unchanged at about 19.9 million due to population growth. By contrast, Southern Asia combined a 6.1% decline in the under-five population with a fall in stunting prevalence from 40.3% to 31.4%, reducing the number of stunted children from 77.0 million to 56.4 million between 2012 and 2024.

Data limitations must also be considered for interpretation. These rankings use JME modeled stunting estimates, which are appropriate for trend comparison but produce smoothed trajectories rather than direct annual observations. Acute shocks – including conflict, economic crisis, and food emergencies – may not be captured in real time by the modeled series. Estimates and rankings should not be over-interpreted.
